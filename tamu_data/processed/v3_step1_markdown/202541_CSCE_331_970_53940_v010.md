[16.0pt] Course Description
Intensive programming experience and provision of the fundamentals needed for
larger-scale software development; integration of concepts in computer science
and familiarization with a variety of programming and development tools and
techniques; team projects each with an emphasis on a different specialization
within computer science; emphasis on programming techniques to ease code
integration and clarity; practical exposure to software-engineering processes
through large-scale projects and specification and documentation.
[16.0pt] Course Prerequisites
Prerequisite/Corequisite(s):  Grade of C or better in CSCE 314,
CSCE 350/ECEN 350, or ECEN 350/CSCE 350; grade of C or better or concurrent
enrollment in CSCE 313.
If you have not yet mastered the concepts in these courses, you should visit me or a

later CSCE technical courses.

[16.0pt] Course Learning Outcomes
This course is an introduction to software engineering. It will familiarize you with a
variety of software development methodologies. We will cover many topics
including software system design for portability, performance, and testability,
coding layout and style, programming specifications and documentation, software
tools and API usage, databases, and interface design. You will practice these
concepts primarily through small team projects to help you grow in your
programming and software design skills. Many students discover a new topic area
they enjoy, whether that be front-end development, back-end development, project
management, or a particular technology.
Upon completion of this course, you will be able to:
Explain  the need for software engineering through industry examples and
personal experience
Exercise  the fundamental concepts of software construction (including
managing requirements, design, implementation, testing, and deployment)
Design and develop  software that is clearer, more maintainable, and
integrates current software technologies (some that are new to you)
Collaborate and communicate  effectively in small teams
Recognize and apply  characteristics of effective technical writing
If you enjoy the topics explored in this course, consider taking CSCE 431: Software
Engineering for a deeper understanding of software development and project
management.

[16.0pt] Grading Policy
Your course grade reflects your mastery of the course material by the end of the
course. It has multiple components to facilitate your learning and growth.
Throughout the course, you will receive feedback to see how you are doing. This

[12.9pt] Grading Components
Foundational Course Activities – 10%:  The course has several different
types of foundational activities to help you better understand concepts,
connect with the material, and support your longer-term learning as you
work, both individually and with other students. Foundational activities
include at-home reading activities (15%), in-class activities (25%), in-class recall
practices (10%), in-lab activities (25%), and team success contributions (25%).
Notes:
You must be physically present for in-class activities, in-class recall
practices, and in-lab activities to receive credit.
For group assignments, your full participation is required to receive
credit.
For submitted assignments, grading will be based on the quality of the
submitted work.
For reading assignments, each and every Perusall assignment must be
accessed from Canvas to receive credit and for grades to transfer to
Canvas.
The team success contribution is your individual contribution to the
success of the team projects and group assignments. In any team
environment, striving to be a valuable team member is critical. Logged
code commits, peer evaluation, attendance, participation, and
instructor/TA observations will be evaluated for this grade. Failure to
honestly and accurately participate in the peer evaluation process
(giving evaluations and/or reflecting on evaluations) can negatively
impact your individual contribution score. An example of not
participating honestly and accurately in peer evaluations is to give all
top scores (like all 5's on a 0-5 scale) when the project is behind, lacking
in quality, or a member promises to catch up for a better evaluation.
Individual Project – 5%:  A project about HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. This
project is completed prior to the team projects.
Team Project 1– 16%:  A project about databases. You will gain experience
using the Waterfall Software Development Model. You will have multiple
incremental assessments (30%) to receive feedback so you can make
adjustments in your effort level and approach in addition to the overall
assessment of the delivered product (70%).
Team Project 2 – 36%:  A continuation of Team Project 1 which adds cloud
and web programming. You will gain experience using the Agile/Scrum
Software Development Model. As with Team Project 1, you will have multiple
incremental assessments (30%) to receive feedback so you can make
adjustments in your effort level and approach in addition to the overall
assessment of the delivered product (70%).
Written Elements – 33%:  This course provides writing instruction and
multiple opportunities to develop and practice your skills. You will also receive
formative feedback from which you can revise your writing.  As a writing-
intensive course, you must pass the writing components with a C or
better to pass the course.  Many of these writing assignments are integrated
into the projects.
[12.9pt] Assignment Assessment
Specific grading practices will be announced when assignments are given through
assignment descriptions and assignment rubrics. In the software industry,
prototypes must satisfy a minimum viable solution to be accepted by the client. In
the same way,  assignment submissions in this course must meet a minimum
viable solution  to receive credit.
The minimum viable solution is defined as a good faith effort resulting in a
submission that matches the requirements of the assignment and scores at
least 50%. Submissions that fail to meet this standard will receive a 0.
Keep in mind the rubric is not the assignment and may be adjusted to more
closely reflect the items being assessed. Always follow the assignment
description.

[12.9pt] AI Policy Statement for Software Engineering
Artificial Intelligence (AI) tools -- such as ChatGPT, Copilot, and others -- are rapidly
changing the practice of software engineering. In this course, you will be both
required and encouraged to use AI in structured, intentional ways to deepen your
understanding of how these tools affect your workflow and outputs. The course
policy below is intended to help you learn how to use modern AI tools responsibly
and ethically in software engineering.
Required Uses of AI
Certain assignments will direct you to use AI tools for specific tasks (e.g., generating
code comments, comparing your code to AI-generated code, or improving writing).
Follow all instructions for these assignments precisely, including any prompt
engineering or documentation requirements.
When AI tools are required for an assignment, you must cite the tool used, include
your prompts and the AI’s responses, and reflect on the results as instructed.
Permitted Uses of AI
AI may be used to assist with limited, well-defined tasks (such as debugging or
suggesting code comments), but may not be used to design your entire project or
to complete assignments whose primary learning outcomes involve your own
design and implementation.
Direct use of AI to generate complete solutions for programming assignments,
project designs, or other major tasks is prohibited unless explicitly stated otherwise
in the assignment instructions.
AI and Academic Integrity Requirements
If you do use AI in any part of your work, including explicit assignment directions,
you must clearly cite the tool, document prompts, and output, and explain how you
used it. Failure to do so will be considered academic misconduct and will be
reported to the  Aggie Honor System Office .
Be aware: misusing AI can limit your learning and preparation for assessments.
Historically, students who over-relied on AI performed poorly on independent
assessments.
If you are uncertain whether a particular use of AI is allowed, ask the instructor
before proceeding.
