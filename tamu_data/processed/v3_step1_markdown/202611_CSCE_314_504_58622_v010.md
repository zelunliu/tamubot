[16.0pt] Catalog Description
Programming Languages. (3-0). Credit 3. Exploration of the design space of
programming languages via an in-depth study of two programming languages, one
functional and one object-oriented; focuses on idiomatic uses of each language and
on features characteristic for each language. Prerequisite: Grade of C or better in
CSCE 221, or concurrent enrollment.
[16.0pt] Course Prerequisites
Prerequisite/Corequisite(s):  Grade of C or better in CSCE 221, or concurrent
enrollment.
Technical Skills Expectations:
Students should be familiar with installing libraries/packages, configuring
environments, executing the compilers, and running compiled programs (with or
without an IDE). Familiarity with the Linux environment is needed. Experience with
Git is expected.
Prerequisites Waiver:
Students who wish to enroll in the course without the prerequisites should contact
the Undergraduate Advising Office at  undergraduate-advisors@cse.tamu.edu , not
the instructor.
Course Auditing:
Auditing a course must go through a formal approval process per student rules.
This is often for very rare extenuating scenarios, i.e., the reason cannot be to
prepare to take the class next semester, or to effectively retake a course without
being enrolled.
[16.0pt] Course Learning Outcomes
Upon completion of this course, the student will be able to:
Use modern programming languages effectively
Develop the necessary skills to learn and adopt new programming languages
Evaluate the power of programming languages and their constructs
Apply language constructs, common abstraction mechanisms, and efficiency
considerations
Analyze the meaning and efficiency of functional and object-oriented
programs
Master many specific language concepts and use them effectively to build
correct and elegant software
Although the course uses and studies specific programming languages, learning
these languages should be considered a side benefit of the course, not the main
goal.

most one can score in an assignment is 10. At 39 hours late, the

[12.9pt] Course Evaluation
Evaluations are a way for students to provide feedback regarding their instructor
and the course after the delivery, including student perceptions of learning and the
student experience in the course. Detailed feedback will enable the instructor to
continuously tailor and improve teaching methods and course content to meet the
course’s learning goals and the students’ academic needs. They are a requirement
of the course and are key to continuing to provide students with the highest quality
of teaching.
Texas A&M University utilizes HelioCampus-Assessment, formerly AEFIS, to collect
student course evaluations. The evaluations are anonymous; the instructor and
administration do not track who entered what responses. At no point in the
process that the instructor knows the students’ identity or whether a student has
completed the evaluation. Students are strongly encouraged to participate in the
course evaluation. Refer to Office of Institutional Effectiveness & Evaluation page for
more information. The instructor welcomes feedback during the course.
The Faculty Senate passed a Resolution in 2010 (FS.27.122) that specifically stated
"The Faculty Senate opposes granting academic credit of any kind to students for
completing course evaluations ...", this would include incentives such as bonus
points. As such, there will be no credit for completing the course evaluation.

[12.9pt] Plagiarism - Academic Integrity
Plagiarism is one of the worst academic sins, for the plagiarist destroys trust among
colleagues and the purpose of learning. Plagiarism is commonly defined as passing
off as one’s own ideas, words, writings, code, etc., which belong to another person.
By this definition, in this course, a student commits plagiarism if copying the work
elsewhere, even with the permission of the author, and turns it in as one’s own.
The instructor encourages collaboration and the exchange of ideas among
students. Collaboration is important for facilitating learning, and peers often can be
a great resource. However, all assignments in this course are individual efforts. All
assignments must be fully completed by only the student to whom they are
assigned, without unauthorized aid. Any academic dishonesty will not be tolerated
and will be severely dealt with. Consequences include a zero grade for the
activity/assignment, course failure, and dismissal from the university, among others.
Refer to the University's Academic Integrity Statement and Policy. All involved
parties will be penalized equally.
Specifically, students may discuss the assignments at a high level (e.g.,
concepts, approaches) with friends but absolutely no actual solution sharing
(voluntarily or involuntarily) is allowed.
Code: Students are not allowed to use code found elsewhere (e.g., Google
searches, online repositories, past offerings of the course). Students also
must not attempt to circumvent the objective of an assignment (e.g., using a
banned library or a function to bypass the goal of the assignment)
Non-code: The write-up of homeworks must be done individually and must
not be shared in any way or form.
No full or partial deliverables are allowed to be knowingly or unknowingly
released in the public domain even after the deadlines and after the course is
over. Students are solely responsible for securing all deliverables away from
the public.
Group review sessions for exams are allowed and encouraged.
Posting assignments to or copying solutions from unauthorized sources is
academic misconduct.
There have been past incidents where students used code from Chegg or
similar tutoring websites; the use of such resources is not allowed.
Complicity
Every student should understand that complicity – helping or attempting to help
another student commit an act of academic dishonesty – also constitutes academic
dishonesty and carries the same punishment as cheating and plagiarism.
In other words, if providing a solution to another student, even if that student does
not turn it in for credit, the provider has committed an act of academic dishonesty.
Occasionally, we find evidence of plagiarism after the assignments have been
graded. For example, a prior assignment may be flagged when a new detector
becomes available. For these reasons, you should treat your assignment grades as
provisional. Any prior submission that is later found to constitute a violation will be
reported to the Aggie Honor System Office (AHSO).
Use of Generative AI:
The use of AI to gain a greater understanding of a topic is a great use of its
capabilities. This is allowed. However, using AI to complete assignments is a poor
use of its capabilities, as it does not increase a student’s capabilities. This is
prohibited.
Code: While consulting AI tools is allowed, students are prohibited from
submitting AI-generated code for the assignments.
Writing: All non-code submissions must be generated by students
themselves. Students must not have another entity write any portion of an
assignment for them.

[16.0pt] Academic Integrity Statement and Policy
“An Aggie does not lie, cheat or steal, or tolerate those who do.”
“Texas A&M University students are responsible for authenticating all work
submitted to an instructor. If asked, students must be able to produce proof that
the item submitted is indeed the work of that student. Students must keep
appropriate records at all times. The inability to authenticate one’s work, should the
instructor request it, may be sufficient grounds to initiate an academic misconduct
case” (Section 20.1.2.3,  Student Rule 20 ).
You can learn more about the Aggie Honor System Office Rules and Procedures,
academic integrity, and your rights and responsibilities at  aggiehonor.tamu.edu .
[16.0pt] University Attendance Policy
The university views class attendance and participation as an individual student
responsibility. Students are expected to attend class and to complete all
assignments.
Please refer to  Student Rule 7 i n its entirety for information about excused
absences, including definitions, and related documentation and timelines.
[16.0pt] Makeup Work Policy
Students will be excused from attending class on the day of a graded activity or
when attendance contributes to a student’s grade, for the reasons stated in
Student Rule 7, or other reason deemed appropriate by the instructor.
Please refer to  Student Rule 7  in its entirety for information about makeup work,
including definitions, and related documentation and timelines.
Absences related to Title IX of the Education Amendments of 1972 may necessitate
a period of more than 30 days for make-up work, and the timeframe for make-up
work should be agreed upon by the student and instructor” ( Student Rule 7, Section
7.4.1 ).
“The instructor is under no obligation to provide an opportunity for the student to
make up work missed because of an unexcused absence” ( Student Rule 7, Section
7.4.2 ).
Students who request an excused absence are expected to uphold the Aggie
Honor Code and Student Conduct Code. ( See Student Rule 24 .)

[16.0pt] Pregnancy Accommodations
Texas A&M provides reasonable accommodations to students due to pregnancy
and/or related conditions, such as childbirth, recovery, and lactation. Students
should contact the University’s  Pregnancy Coordinator  as soon as they become
aware of the need for accommodation. Depending on the circumstances,
accommodations could include extended time to complete assignments or exams,
changes in course sequence, or modifications to the physical classroom
environment.
Texas A&M will also allow a voluntary leave of absence, ensure the availability of
lactation space, and maintain grievance procedures to provide for the prompt and
equitable resolution of complaints of sex discrimination. For information regarding
pregnancy accommodations, email  TIX.Pregnancy@tamu.edu .

[16.0pt] Privacy Act (FERPA)
FERPA is a federal law designed to protect the privacy of educational records by
limiting access to these records, to establish the right of students to inspect and
review their educational records, and to provide guidelines for the correction of
inaccurate and misleading data through informal and formal hearings.
Currently enrolled students wishing to withhold any or all directory information
items can do so within  howdy.tamu.edu  using the Directory Information
Withholding Form. The complete  FERPA Notice to Students  and the student records
policy is available on the Office of the Registrar webpage.
Items that can never be identified as public information are a student’s social
security number, citizenship, gender, grades, GPR, or class schedule. All efforts will
be made in this class to protect your privacy and to ensure confidential treatment
of information associated with or generated by your participation in the class.
Directory items include name, UIN, local address, permanent address, email
address, local telephone number, permanent telephone number, dates of
attendance, program of study (college, major, campus), classification, previous
institutions attended, degrees, honors and awards received, participation in
officially recognized activities and sports, medical residence location, and medical
residence specialization.
[16.0pt] Free Speech and Civil Discourse
Texas A&M recognizes that the pursuit of truth through open and robust discourse
is critical to academic inquiry. However, as a community of scholars, the university
has an aspirational expectation that such discourse will be conducted in
accordance with Aggie Core Values. In this “marketplace of ideas,” we encourage
civil dialogue creating an environment that allows individuals to express their ideas

learn more about Freedom of Expression and Free Speech on the  University's
