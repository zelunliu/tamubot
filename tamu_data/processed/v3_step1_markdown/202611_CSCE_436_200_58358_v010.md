[16.0pt] Catalog Description
Computer-Human Interaction. (3-0). Credit 3. Comprehensive study of the
Computer-Human Interaction (CHI) area; includes history and importance of CHI;
CHI design theories; modeling of computer users and interfaces; empirical
techniques for task analysis and interface design; styles of interaction and future
directions of CHI including hypermedia and computer-supported collaborative
work. Prerequisites: Grade of C or better in CSCE 315 or CSCE 331 or concurrent
enrollment; or approval of instructor.
[16.0pt] Additional Course Details
[12.9pt] Course Description
Introduces foundational theories and models of human-computer interaction (HCI).
Explores modeling users cognitively and socially, as well as methods for gathering
and analyzing qualitative and quantitative data on individuals and groups.
Addresses the development of requirements for effective interaction design using
evidence from user needs, context, and data analysis. Provides hands-on
experience applying HCI principles to design, prototype, implement, and evaluate
interactive systems. Includes opportunities to communicate design decisions and
findings using various formats, and to critique and discuss current practices and
trends within the field.

[12.9pt] Grade Weighting
Pre-Class Engagement (10%)
Pre-class engagement can include but is not limited to course readings, quizzes,
polls, and surveys.
You get credit by submitting engagement submissions prior to the start of
class.
At the end of the semester, we will calculate your completion rate and add
15% capped at 100%.
This allows you to miss some pre-class engagements without negatively
impacting your score. For example, a friend has a personal crisis, and
you choose to console them instead of doing some pre-class
engagements. You do not have to ask the instructor for special
consideration since it is built into the course.
Group Class Activities (Section 2XX: 5%, Section 5XX: 10%)
You will work with people at your table or move to a table so that there are at least
3 people per group.
You will get credit if you attend class and you provide what is required for the
activity.
At the end of the semester, we will calculate your completion rate and add
15% capped at 100%.
This allows you to miss some group class activities without negatively
impacting your score. For example, you had a flat tire on the way to
campus that caused you to miss a group class activity. You do not have
to ask the instructor for special consideration since it is built into the
course.
Assignments (20%)
You will complete assignments throughout the semester either individually or with
others as specified in the assignment. Note that assignments done with others are
not a part of the group project.
Some assignments will be peer graded.
1. Your score will be based on peer evaluations as well as your evaluation
of other student assignments.
You can lose points for grading significantly differently than your
peers or giving all students the same scores. (E.g. You will need to
grade critically rather than give everyone full credit.)
Current Research Presentation (10%)
You will present a summary of HCI research.
Your presentation will be based on one paper from an HCI conference or
journal.
Your presentation will be peer graded.
Grading
This will be peer graded.
Your score will be based on peer evaluations as well as your evaluation
of other student assignments.
You can lose points for grading significantly differently than your
peers or giving all students the same scores. (E.g. You will need to
grade critically rather than give everyone full credit.)
Quick Applications (20%)
Timed assignments that have you do quick application of class materials. They are
designed to take 15 to 20 minutes while you will have at least 45 minutes to
complete them.
Team Project (30%)
You will work with a group on an in-depth HCI-based project on a topic selected by
your team with approval of the instructor.
Parts of this will be peer graded.
1. Your score will be based on peer evaluations as well as your evaluation
of other student assignments.
You can lose points for grading significantly differently than your
peers or giving all students the same scores. (E.g. You will need to
grade critically rather than give everyone full credit.)
The instructor will establish a multiplier for you based on your team peer
evaluations.
1. The instructor can modify the multiplier at their discretion.
For example, if you are not actively participating with your group
and you are assigned a multiplier of 0.9 and your group’s final
project score is 90, then your score would become 0.9*90 which
is 81.
Honors Activities (Section 2XX: 5%, Section 5XX: 0%)
Only students in honors sections.
You will develop and submit two artifacts that help to deepen your
understanding of a course topic.
You will review other student artifacts and provide feedback.

Accessibility Statement
Howdy! Your CSCE 489 instructors want you to succeed in this challenging course.
While the  Disability Accommodation Portal (AIM Portal)  makes it easier to share
accommodation information, we ask that you also schedule a meeting with your
section’s instructor to discuss your accommodations in person. In addition to
accommodations, we’re happy to talk about strategies for succeeding in this course.
We strongly encourage you to secure your accommodations—even if you’re unsure
you’ll need them. When Dr. Moore began teaching at Texas A&M, he noticed a
concerning pattern: students often chose to take the first exam without using their
approved accommodations. Many said they wanted to “try it without them.” After
the first exam, there was often a rush of accommodation requests. These instances
have decreased significantly since we've been more explicit about our preferences.
While Dr. Moore suspects that some students hesitate due to negative experiences
with previous instructors, we want you to know that our department culture is one
of support. We are committed to providing accommodations approved by Disability
Resources with professionalism and care.
“We prefer that you get your accommodations and not need them, rather than
trying without your accommodations and finding out that you needed them.”
Finally, everyone should be mindful of creating software and digital artifacts that are
accessible. If you're majoring in computer science or computer engineering,
consider taking CSCE 432: Accessible Computing, a course developed by Dr. Moore
to help students design with accessibility in mind.

[16.0pt] AI Statement
With the rise of artificial intelligence (AI) technologies, our creative processes are
evolving. However, these tools do not replace the essential skills of drafting, revising,
and reflecting on texts, programs, and lectures. Nor do they substitute for the
critical thinking required to locate, evaluate, incorporate, and cite sources
responsibly.
Education is about preparing future innovators and leaders. That includes learning
to use emerging technologies ethically and effectively. In this course, you are
permitted to use generative AI tools (e.g., ChatGPT, Google Bard, DALL·E 2) to assist
with assignments if you choose to. These tools can help you brainstorm ideas or
provide feedback, but they should not complete your work for you.
To maintain academic integrity:
Cite any AI-generated  content that informs your work. This includes in-text
citations, quotations, and entries in your reference list.
Failure to properly attribute AI-generated material is considered
academic dishonesty .
No more than 30% of any assignment  should consist of AI-generated text.
For guidance on citing  generative AI  using APA 7th edition, refer to the APA Style
Blog for examples, including how to cite ChatGPT in both the body of your work and
your reference list.
Please be aware of the limitations of generative AI:
It often recycles existing ideas rather than producing original thought.
It may present false or misleading information alongside accurate content.
It can hallucinate—creating citations or facts that do not exist.
It may restructure your writing in ways that distort your intended meaning.
Always review and critically evaluate any AI-generated content before including it in
your work.

[16.0pt] Privacy Act (FERPA)
FERPA is a federal law designed to protect the privacy of educational records by
limiting access to these records, to establish the right of students to inspect and
review their educational records, and to provide guidelines for the correction of
inaccurate and misleading data through informal and formal hearings.
Currently enrolled students wishing to withhold any or all directory information
items can do so within  howdy.tamu.edu  using the Directory Information
Withholding Form. The complete  FERPA Notice to Students  and the student records
policy is available on the Office of the Registrar webpage.
Items that can never be identified as public information are a student’s social
security number, citizenship, gender, grades, GPR, or class schedule. All efforts will
be made in this class to protect your privacy and to ensure confidential treatment
of information associated with or generated by your participation in the class.
Directory items include name, UIN, local address, permanent address, email
address, local telephone number, permanent telephone number, dates of
attendance, program of study (college, major, campus), classification, previous
institutions attended, degrees, honors and awards received, participation in
officially recognized activities and sports, medical residence location, and medical
residence specialization.
