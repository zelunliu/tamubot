[14.8pt] Course Description
Intensive programming experience and provision of the fundamentals needed for larger-
scale software development; integration of concepts in computer science and
familiarization with a variety of programming and development tools and techniques; team
projects each with an emphasis on a different specialization within computer science;
emphasis on programming techniques to ease code integration and clarity; practical
exposure to software-engineering processes through large-scale projects and specification
and documentation.
[14.8pt] Course Prerequisites
Prerequisite/Corequisite(s):  Grade of C or better in CSCE 314, CSCE 350/ECEN 350, or
ECEN 350/CSCE 350; grade of C or better or concurrent enrollment in CSCE 313.
If you have not yet mastered the concepts in these courses, you should visit me or a TA. We
can provide resources to help you learn and review them. Mastery of prerequisite concepts
is important as this course will build on that knowledge and grow your understanding of
programming and computer systems.
For students taking CSCE 313 concurrently:  If you decide to q-drop CSCE 313 to defer it
to a future semester, you will not be able to remain in this course. Both courses are needed
to satisfy the curriculum's overall design and to prepare you for later CSCE technical
courses.

W (writing-intensive course):

This course is a writing-intensive (W) course. You will practice the types of writing you will

[14.8pt] Course Learning Outcomes
This course is an introduction to software engineering. It will familiarize you with a variety of
software development methodologies. We will cover many topics including software system
design for portability, performance, and testability, coding layout and style, programming
specifications and documentation, software tools and API usage, databases, and interface
design. You will practice these concepts primarily through small team projects to help you
grow in your programming and software design skills. Many students discover a new topic
area they enjoy, whether that be front-end development, back-end development, project
management, or a particular technology.
Upon completion of this course, the learner will be able to:
Explain  the need for software engineering through industry examples and personal
experience
Exercise  the fundamental concepts of software construction (including managing
requirements, design, implementation, testing, and deployment)
Design and develop  software that is clearer, more maintainable, and integrates
current software technologies (some that are new to you)
Collaborate and communicate  effectively in small teams
Recognize and apply  characteristics of effective technical writing

[14.8pt] Grading Policy
Your course grade reflects your mastery of the course material by the end of the course. It
has multiple components to facilitate your learning and growth. Throughout the course,
you will receive feedback to see how you are doing. This feedback can show you where to
apply more effort, use new strategies, or seek help. Projects begin with smaller
scope/weight and increase throughout the semester as you progress in your learning.
Grading Components
Foundational Course Activities – 10%:  The course has several different types of
foundational activities to help you better understand concepts, connect with the
material, and support your longer-term learning as you work, both individually and
with other students. Foundational activities include at-home reading activities (15%),
in-class activities (25%), in-class recall practices (10%), in-lab activities (25%), and team
success contributions (25%).
Notes:
You must be physically present for in-class activities, in-class recall practices,
and in-lab activities to receive credit.
For group assignments, your full participation is required to receive credit.
For submitted assignments, grading will be based on the quality of the
submitted work.
For reading assignments, each and every Perusall assignment must be
accessed from Canvas to receive credit and for grades to transfer to Canvas.
The team success contribution is your individual contribution to the success of
the team projects and group assignments. In any team environment, striving to
be a valuable team member is critical. Logged code commits, peer evaluation,
attendance, participation, and instructor/TA observations will be evaluated for
this grade. Failure to honestly and accurately participate in the peer evaluation
process (giving evaluations and/or reflecting on evaluations) can negatively
impact your individual contribution score. An example of not participating
honestly and accurately in peer evaluations is to give all top scores (like all 5's
on a 0-5 scale) when the project is behind, lacking in quality, or a member
promises to catch up for a better evaluation.
Individual Project – 5%:  A project about HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. This project is
completed prior to the team projects.
Team Project 1– 16%:  A project about databases. You will gain experience using the
Waterfall Software Development Model. You will have multiple incremental
assessments (30%) to receive feedback so you can make adjustments in your effort
level and approach in addition to the overall assessment of the delivered product
(70%).
Team Project 2 – 36%:  A continuation of Team Project 1 which adds cloud and web
programming. You will gain experience using the Agile/Scrum Software Development
Model. As with Team Project 1, you will have multiple incremental assessments (30%)
to receive feedback so you can make adjustments in your effort level and approach in
addition to the overall assessment of the delivered product (70%).
Written Elements – 33%:  This course provides writing instruction and multiple
opportunities to develop and practice your skills. You will also receive formative
feedback from which you can revise your writing.  As a writing-intensive course, you
must pass the writing components with a C or better to pass the course.  Many
of these writing assignments are integrated into the projects.
Assignment Assessment
Specific grading practices will be announced when assignments are given through
assignment descriptions and assignment rubrics. In the software industry, prototypes must
satisfy a minimum viable solution to be accepted by the client. In the same way,
assignment submissions in this course must meet a minimum viable solution  to
receive credit.
The minimum viable solution is defined as a good faith effort resulting in a
submission that matches the requirements of the assignment and scores at least
50%. Submissions that fail to meet this standard will receive a 0.
Keep in mind the rubric is not the assignment and may be adjusted to more closely
reflect the items being assessed. Always follow the assignment description.
Grading Scale
Grades will be assigned according to the following scale:
A  ≥  90 > B  ≥  80 > C  ≥  70 > D  ≥  60 > F
These grades represent varying degrees of achievement (see the  university's grading
system ):
A = excellent, B = good, C = satisfactory, D = passing, F = failing
Letter Grade Reduction for Failing a Grading Component or Excessive Absences
Students who earn less than 70% credit for any grading component (foundational course
activities, projects, team success component, or written elements) have not mastered the
course. They will receive a letter grade reduction.
Lab sessions are an essential time for working with your team. Missing 6 or more lab
sessions (which constitutes 20% oscheduled labs) will result in a reduction of your final
course letter grade.

misleading data through informal and formal hearings.

[14.8pt] AI Statement
AI Policy Statement for Software Engineering
Artificial Intelligence (AI) tools -- such as ChatGPT, Copilot, and others -- are rapidly changing
the practice of software engineering. In this course, you will be both required and
encouraged to use AI in structured, intentional ways to deepen your understanding of how
these tools affect your workflow and outputs. The course policy below is intended to help
you learn how to use modern AI tools responsibly and ethically in software engineering.
Required Uses of AI
Certain assignments will direct you to use AI tools for specific tasks (e.g., generating code
comments, comparing your code to AI-generated code, or improving writing). Follow all
instructions for these assignments precisely, including any prompt engineering or
documentation requirements.
When AI tools are required for an assignment, you must cite the tool used, include your
prompts and the AI’s responses, and reflect on the results as instructed.
Permitted Uses of AI
AI may be used to assist with limited, well-defined tasks (such as debugging or suggesting
code comments), but may not be used to design your entire project or to complete
assignments whose primary learning outcomes involve your own design and
implementation.
Direct use of AI to generate complete solutions for programming assignments, project
designs, or other major tasks is prohibited unless explicitly stated otherwise in the
assignment instructions.
AI and Academic Integrity Requirements
If you do use AI in any part of your work, including explicit assignment directions, you must
clearly cite the tool, document prompts, and output, and explain how you used it. Failure to
do so will be considered academic misconduct and will be reported to the  Aggie Honor
System Office .
Be aware: misusing AI can limit your learning and preparation for assessments. Historically,
students who over-relied on AI performed poorly on independent assessments.
If you are uncertain whether a particular use of AI is allowed, ask the instructor before
proceeding.
