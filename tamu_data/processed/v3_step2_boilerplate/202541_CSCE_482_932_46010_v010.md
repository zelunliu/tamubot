[16.0pt] Course Description
This is a project-based course focusing on skills for system integration in order to
solve real-world problems in computer science. It involves a significant team
software project that integrates advanced concepts across computer science
specializations, requiring the whole process from design, implementation,
documentation and demonstration, as well as establishing a design methodology,
management process and team management. Emphasis is placed upon student's
activities as design professionals.
Detailed Description
CSCE 482 is a project-oriented course aimed at developing system integration skills.
Students work in groups of 3-6 people to complete a significant software
engineering design project. Every project requires complete implementation,
documentation and demonstration of a software system, which may also involve
minor hardware too. The focus is not only on the final product but also on design
methodology, management process and teamwork.
Each team will be required to manage its own efforts to complete its project in a
timely manner. Group members will be required to keep individual lab notebooks
recording their efforts and their personal impressions of the project. Students will
be graded based on both the quality of the group product and their individual
contributions.
Every team will be required to schedule a weekly meeting with the course
instructor and the TA, preferably during the official class or lab hours. These
meetings must be attended by every group member. Since the projects will be
student managed, the exact nature and style of these meetings is at the group’s
discretion. However, every member of the group is expected to participate. At the
end of the semester, each group will make a public presentation describing and
demonstrating their work. These presentations will be open to the university
community and visitors from industry.

[16.0pt] Course Learning Outcomes
Course Objectives
To prepare students for engineering practice with a major design experience based
on the knowledge and skills acquired in earlier course work and incorporating
standards and realistic constraints that include most of the following
considerations: economic; environmental; sustainability; manufacturability; ethical;
health and safety; social; and political.
Course Learning Outcomes
It is expected that successful participation in the course will allow the student to
demonstrate:
• an ability to apply knowledge of mathematics, science, and engineering (3.a)
• an ability to design and conduct experiments, as well as to analyze and interpret
data (3.b)
• an ability to design a system, component, or process to meet desired needs (3.c)
• an ability to function on multi-disciplinary teams (3.d)
• an ability to identify, formulate, and solve engineering problems (3.e)
• an understanding of professional and ethical responsibility (3.f)
• an ability to communicate effectively (3.g)
• an ability to use the techniques, skills, and modern engineering tools necessary
for engineering practice (3.k)

[16.0pt] Grading Policy
The final grade you will receive in the class will be based on points accumulated
during the semester. Thus, both continued progress (the process) and the quality of
your product (and other deliverables) will determine your grade. Although the
majority of your grade (55%) is based on the performance of your team, individual
performance will also be closely evaluated.
The grading scale is:
A 90-100
B 80-89
C 70-79
D 60-69
F 59 or below.
[12.9pt bold] Detailed break-down
[10.5pt] The following provides a break-down of the components of the grade.
1. Project Proposal (10%)
These points will be based on the originality, quality and feasibility of
the proposed work, the analysis of alternative solutions, the
consideration of societal/economic/ethical aspects, and the project
management approach, as reflected on your written proposal (75%)
and oral presentation (25%). Submission of the written document will
be due 11:59 P.M.
2. Critical Design Review (10%)
The CDR is a mid-semester evaluation of your project. The grade will be
based on your progress to date, and the quality of your oral
presentation and accompanying report.  Submission of the written
document will be due 11:59 P.M.
3. Final Communication (10% )
[10.5pt] This grade will be based on the quality of the final presentation (25%), as
[10.5pt] well as the contents and professional finish of the documentation (75%).
[10.5pt] Final reports should have a discussion of constraints that the team had to
[10.5pt] satisfy (e.g., cost, time, technology limitations) and of relevant industry
[10.5pt] standards used (e.g., coding, interfaces, safety). This includes a video
[10.5pt] describing the project. Submission of the written document will be due
[10.5pt] 11:59 P.M.
4. Project Grade: Final completion, Weekly Progress, and Team Effectiveness
(25% )
[10.5pt] A final grade will be assigned to your project based on the completion of all
[10.5pt] the objectives stated in the proposal, as well as on a live demonstration.
[10.5pt] The complexity of your project and the size of your team will be factored in.
[10.5pt] Due date: Project demonstrations will take place the last day of class of the
[10.5pt] week prior to final presentations. This earlier deadline ensures that teams
[10.5pt] have time to (i) thoroughly test and validate their systems after the demo,
[10.5pt] and (ii) prepare the final presentation.
[10.5pt] The weekly progress part of this grade will be based on your team’s ability
[10.5pt] to keep the project on schedule. The weekly report should be incremental,
[10.5pt] and should specifically address the following:
[10.5pt bold] a. An  agenda for the meeting  with the instructor/TA.
[10.5pt bold] b. Any  major accomplishments  during this time period, including
[10.5pt] figures and results.
[10.5pt bold] c. The  team’s goals  for the following week.
[10.5pt] d. An update on project management, including teamwork, purchases,
[10.5pt] schedule and milestone status.
[10.5pt bold] e.  Minutes  of the previous meeting.
[10.5pt] Weekly progress reports are due 1:30 P.M. the day of the weekly meeting.
[10.5pt] This is due only for those weeks in which a meeting is held. The
[10.5pt] responsibility of preparing these reports will be rotated among team
[10.5pt] members. The team member preparing the report will also be in charge of
[10.5pt] facilitating the discussions during that weekly meeting, and preparing an
[10.5pt] action list for the following week.
[10.5pt] It is very important to understand that accomplishing the technical
[10.5pt] objectives of the project is not sufficient. These accomplishments should not
[10.5pt] come at the expense of destroying relationships among team members.
[10.5pt] Thus, a grade will be assigned based on the ability of the group to function
[10.5pt] as a team. Is there evidence that the group engaged in team building
[10.5pt] activities? Were contributions to the project evenly distributed? Were
[10.5pt] members equally engaged in discussions during meetings? Was there an
[10.5pt] effective division of responsibilities?
5.  Individual Performance (45%)
[10.5pt] Points in this category are based on assessment of your personal
[10.5pt] contribution to the project and team-effort:
[10.5pt] a. Personal Design and Implementation Contributions (35%)
[10.5pt] You are required record what YOU do as a member of the
[10.5pt] project. This can be done in virtual form, via software version
[10.5pt] control comments and notes/commentary on the log. An
[10.5pt] example of a commentary of a git log will be provided; this
[10.5pt] may need to be supplemented with extra documentation (e.g.,
[10.5pt] showing sketches and design alternatives explored which did
[10.5pt] not make it into the final project).
[10.5pt] A grade will be assigned to your personal contribution and logs
[10.5pt] of your design and implementation effort based on:
[10.5pt] 1. The regularity of your entries throughout the semester.
[10.5pt] 2. The evidence of an engineering design process,
[10.5pt] including but not limited to schematics, ER diagrams,
[10.5pt] flow-charts, pseudo-code, tables of experimental
[10.5pt] results, and mathematical derivations.
[10.5pt] 3. The clarity, legibility and organization of your
[10.5pt] annotations and commentary.
[10.5pt] b. Participation (5%) (and potentially Team Dysfunctions exercise)
[10.5pt] The instructor and TA will evaluate your attendance to
[10.5pt] meetings, participation in the discussions, and contributions to
[10.5pt] the team. Team leaders will be evaluated on their efforts to
[10.5pt] facilitate group operation as an effective team.
[10.5pt] Location: Unlike some other capstone offerings, we do not
[10.5pt] insist on work being done in the lab; what matters is that the
[10.5pt] team worked cohesively. Your group is expected to be present
[10.5pt] in the lab on the group’s respective meeting day (typically
[10.5pt] exclusively Tuesday or Thursday), as well as the presentation
[10.5pt] and CDR presentation days.
[10.5pt] c. Peer Review (4%)
[10.5pt] Your performance will be evaluated by each of your team
[10.5pt] members throughout the semester. (Dates appear in the table
[10.5pt] below.)
[10.5pt] d. Ethics Module (1%)
[10.5pt] This is a canvas model and is to be completed on your own
[10.5pt] some time before the final submission.
[10.5pt] Furthermore, the instructor reserves the right to impose a grade penalty if a member is
[10.5pt] seen to actively undermine their group.
Any academic misconduct in this course will result in a grade of F*.
Note: Grades will not be assigned until all project deliverables have been turned in
(see below), all borrowed items (e.g., keys, books, equipment) have been returned

[12.9pt bold] Final Deliverables (i.e., a checklist)

2. A shared drive that includes the following (please organize into folders, e.g.,
Docs, Source, Hardware (if applicable), Media, References, Freeware)

b. Reports: proposal, CDR, weekly reports, final report, and  all

c. Audiovisual media: a ±40 second project overview video as well as a
high-quality video demonstration of the system working.
3. Final hardware prototype, if appropriate, as well as any spare parts and
supplies.
4. Software install, if appropriate, to be demonstrated on multiple machines.
5. The final peer review.
6. Evidence of individual design contributions.
[12.9pt] Document Preparation
All major documents (technical survey, proposal, CDR, and final documentation)
should be submitted in a professional form. This includes containing a title page, an
outline, as well as clear section and subsection headings, etc. The final report
should be bound. Students are strongly encouraged to submit documents typeset
in LaTeX. Proofread and run a spell check before submission! (Note the
opportunities for extra-credit through use of the UWC, mentioned below.)

[16.0pt] Course Specific Attendance Policy
Not attending weekly meetings harms the other members of your group and makes
it much more difficult for the instructor and TA to assess your contributions to the
group effort. Therefore, attendance, punctuality and active participation in the
weekly meetings are strict requirements. Failure to attend a meeting or late arrivals
(more than 5 minutes late) will be reflected in the participation portion of your
individual grade. Emergencies, however, do happen. Lateness or absence can be
excused if there is a valid reason. Illness, job interviews out of town, death in the
family, inclement weather or accidents for commuters, etc., are valid reasons.
Oversleeping, a term paper due, an exam to cram for, etc., are not valid reasons.
Ultimately, the instructor reserves the right to determine what constitutes a “valid
reason” on a case by case basis. If you know you’re going to be late or miss a class,
please let the instructor and your teammates know, so that they may plan for your
absence and make the best use of their time. Attending the career fair is not a basis
for missing class.
[16.0pt] AI Statement
Use of AI tools, including ChatGPT, is permitted for students wishing to use them.
To be consistent with TAMU’s scholarly values, students must cite any AI-generated
material that informed their work and use quotation marks or other appropriate
indicators of quoted material when appropriate. Here is an example of attribution
language: “The author generated this text in part with GPT-4, OpenAI’s large-scale
language-generation model. Upon generating draft language, the author reviewed,
edited, and revised the language to their own liking and takes ultimate responsibility
for the content of this publication.” When substantial parts of the reports were
generated, the relationship between those sections and student contributions
should be discussed by providing additional documentation to accompany the
report submission. For purposes of grading, students may need to defend the
against criticism that your human-authored contributions are limited; this may
require, for instance, providing details of the prompts provided, and/or video
recordings showing your interactions with the AI tools. Students may be asked to
indicate how AI tools informed their process and the influenced final product,
including how you validated any AI-generated citations, which may be invented by
the AI. Claims that have a factual basis and which AI tools have extracted from other
sources, should not be cited merely as attributed to the tool, but require the follow-
up work to (1) verify they were not hallucinated, and (2) to make a proper
attribution. Specifically , “With a current US population of 330 million (ChatGPT-4.0),
the need for X, Y, Z…” is not adequate.
