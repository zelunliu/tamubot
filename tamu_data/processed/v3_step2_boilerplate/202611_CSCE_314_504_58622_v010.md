[16.0pt] Catalog Description
Programming Languages. (3-0). Credit 3. Exploration of the design space of
programming languages via an in-depth study of two programming languages, one
functional and one object-oriented; focuses on idiomatic uses of each language and
on features characteristic for each language. Prerequisite: Grade of C or better in
CSCE 221, or concurrent enrollment.
[16.0pt] Course Prerequisites
Prerequisite/Corequisite(s):  Grade of C or better in CSCE 221, or concurrent
enrollment.
Technical Skills Expectations:
Students should be familiar with installing libraries/packages, configuring
environments, executing the compilers, and running compiled programs (with or
without an IDE). Familiarity with the Linux environment is needed. Experience with
Git is expected.
Prerequisites Waiver:
Students who wish to enroll in the course without the prerequisites should contact
the Undergraduate Advising Office at  undergraduate-advisors@cse.tamu.edu , not
the instructor.
Course Auditing:
Auditing a course must go through a formal approval process per student rules.
This is often for very rare extenuating scenarios, i.e., the reason cannot be to
prepare to take the class next semester, or to effectively retake a course without
being enrolled.
[16.0pt] Course Learning Outcomes
Upon completion of this course, the student will be able to:
Use modern programming languages effectively
Develop the necessary skills to learn and adopt new programming languages
Evaluate the power of programming languages and their constructs
Apply language constructs, common abstraction mechanisms, and efficiency
considerations
Analyze the meaning and efficiency of functional and object-oriented
programs
Master many specific language concepts and use them effectively to build
correct and elegant software
Although the course uses and studies specific programming languages, learning
these languages should be considered a side benefit of the course, not the main
goal.

[12.9pt] Plagiarism - Academic Integrity
Plagiarism is one of the worst academic sins, for the plagiarist destroys trust among
colleagues and the purpose of learning. Plagiarism is commonly defined as passing
off as one’s own ideas, words, writings, code, etc., which belong to another person.
By this definition, in this course, a student commits plagiarism if copying the work
elsewhere, even with the permission of the author, and turns it in as one’s own.
The instructor encourages collaboration and the exchange of ideas among
students. Collaboration is important for facilitating learning, and peers often can be
a great resource. However, all assignments in this course are individual efforts. All
assignments must be fully completed by only the student to whom they are
assigned, without unauthorized aid. Any academic dishonesty will not be tolerated
and will be severely dealt with. Consequences include a zero grade for the
activity/assignment, course failure, and dismissal from the university, among others.
Refer to the University's Academic Integrity Statement and Policy. All involved
parties will be penalized equally.
Specifically, students may discuss the assignments at a high level (e.g.,
concepts, approaches) with friends but absolutely no actual solution sharing
(voluntarily or involuntarily) is allowed.
Code: Students are not allowed to use code found elsewhere (e.g., Google
searches, online repositories, past offerings of the course). Students also
must not attempt to circumvent the objective of an assignment (e.g., using a
banned library or a function to bypass the goal of the assignment)
Non-code: The write-up of homeworks must be done individually and must
not be shared in any way or form.
No full or partial deliverables are allowed to be knowingly or unknowingly
released in the public domain even after the deadlines and after the course is
over. Students are solely responsible for securing all deliverables away from
the public.
Group review sessions for exams are allowed and encouraged.
Posting assignments to or copying solutions from unauthorized sources is
academic misconduct.
There have been past incidents where students used code from Chegg or
similar tutoring websites; the use of such resources is not allowed.
Complicity
Every student should understand that complicity – helping or attempting to help
another student commit an act of academic dishonesty – also constitutes academic
dishonesty and carries the same punishment as cheating and plagiarism.
In other words, if providing a solution to another student, even if that student does
not turn it in for credit, the provider has committed an act of academic dishonesty.
Occasionally, we find evidence of plagiarism after the assignments have been
graded. For example, a prior assignment may be flagged when a new detector
becomes available. For these reasons, you should treat your assignment grades as
provisional. Any prior submission that is later found to constitute a violation will be
reported to the Aggie Honor System Office (AHSO).
Use of Generative AI:
The use of AI to gain a greater understanding of a topic is a great use of its
capabilities. This is allowed. However, using AI to complete assignments is a poor
use of its capabilities, as it does not increase a student’s capabilities. This is
prohibited.
Code: While consulting AI tools is allowed, students are prohibited from
submitting AI-generated code for the assignments.
Writing: All non-code submissions must be generated by students
themselves. Students must not have another entity write any portion of an
assignment for them.
