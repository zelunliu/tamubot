[16.0pt] Course Learning Outcomes
Upon completion of this course, the learner will be able to:
Understand career opportunities and pathways, such as graduate school, for new
graduates in computer science and computer engineering.
Apply techniques discussed when searching for jobs, interviewing, and negotiating.
Analyze ethical implications of professional decisions.

[11.0pt bold] The word count is 150–220 words, with a 30-word buffer; 250 words is a  hard  limit,
[11.0pt bold] and  no exceptions  will be made, even at 251 words.
Concise writing is a key professional skill because technical audiences need clear,
focused communication that respects their limited time. It trains you to prioritize
essential insights, state your main point clearly, and minimize filler, mirroring
expectations for workplace emails and reports.
The strict word limit also supports the course learning outcomes by requiring
specific, meaningful reflections that demonstrate your ability to analyze each
seminar, identify what matters most, and connect it to your own career and ethical
decisions.
Always verify your word count in Microsoft Word or in the Turnitin report (visible
after you submit). Word counts from Canvas, Microsoft Word, and Turnitin are the
only values that will be accepted.
Any evidence that the student did not solely write the reflection (Turnitin score greater
than 30%) is considered academic misconduct and will be reported to the honors
council and result in the application of an appropriate sanction. In addition,
[11.0pt bold] submission of a reflection when not in attendance (in the case of a synchronous
[11.0pt bold] seminar) can constitute academic misconduct.

[16.0pt] AI Statement
Writing: Use of AI tools, including ChatGPT, is permitted in this course for students who wish to
use them. To adhere to our scholarly values, students must cite any AI-generated material that
informed their work (this includes in-text citations and/or use of quotations, and in your
reference list). Using an AI tool to generate content without proper attribution qualifies as
academic dishonesty.
See  https://www.chicagomanualofstyle.org/qanda/data/faq/topics/Documentation/faq0422.
html  for information how to properly cite AI tools. (adapted from University of Massachusetts
Amherst,  https://cte.tamu.edu/getmedia/1d5e4ef6-97f1-4065-987f-3c9dfecbb7bd/TAMU-
CTE_GenAI-SyllabusStatementConsiderations.pdf )
Computer Code. In principle you may submit AI-generated code, or code that is based on or
derived from AI-generated code, as long as this use is properly documented in the comments:
you need to include the prompt and the significant parts of the response. AI tools may help
you avoid syntax errors, but there is no guarantee that the generated code is correct. It is your
responsibility to identify errors in program logic through comprehensive, documented testing.
Moreover, generated code, even if syntactically correct, may have significant scope for
improvement, in particular regarding separation of concerns and avoiding repetitions. The
submission itself must meet our standards of attribution and validation.(from Boris Steipe
(2023) “Syllabus Resources”. The Sentient Syllabus Project  http://sentientsyllabus.org  .)
