[16.0pt] Catalog Description
Senior Capstone Design. (1-6). Credit 3. Development of system integration skills for
solving real-world problems in computer science; significant team software project
that uses integration of advanced concepts across computer science
specializations; projects require design, implementation, documentation and
demonstration, as well as design methodology, management process and
teamwork. Prerequisite: Grade of C or better in CSCE 411, and CSCE 315 or CSCE
331; senior classification; also taught at Galveston campus.
[16.0pt] Course Prerequisites
Prerequisite/Corequisite(s):  Grade of C or better in CSCE 411, and CSCE 315 or
CSCE 331; senior classification; also taught at Galveston campus.
[16.0pt] Course Learning Outcomes
Upon completion of this course, the learner will be able to:
Analyze a complex computing problem and apply principles of computing
and other relevant disciplines to identify solutions.
Design, implement and evaluate a computing-based solution to meet a given
set of computing requirements in the context of the program's discipline.
Communicate effectively in a variety of professional contexts.
Recognize professional responsibilities and make informed judgements in
computing practice based on legal and ethical principles.
Collaborate effectively as a member or leader of a team engaged in activities
appropriate to the program's discipline.
Apply computer science theory and software implementation fundamentals
to produce computing-based solutions.
[16.0pt] Special Course Designation
UCRT - University Required Oral Communication
To receive C credit for this course, you must pass the C component, with the C
course communication requirement below:
A minimum of 35% of your final grade is based on written and oral
communication assignments.
Of this communication component, at least 70% is based on individually
written work and individual public speaking; group work does not count
toward this standard.
To receive C credit, students must pass both the written and public speaking
components of the course. Failure to pass either component will result in
failing the course, regardless of performance on other assignments.
Each student will produce at least 1250 words of individually written work and
deliver a minimum of 5 minutes of individual public speaking.
Assignments fulfilling this requirement include individual progress reports,
project proposals, final technical documentation, and individual presentations
or demonstrations.
Resumes and related materials are accepted only if specific to the major;
take-home essay exams may count if they allow drafting and revision. Oral
presentation slides, scripts, web sites, and posters count when accompanied
by individual presentations.
No more than 10% of the communication grade may be assessed by
undergraduate aides, and all grading by aides is supervised by the instructor.
Failure to meet the individual written or oral communication requirements will
result in failure of the course, regardless of overall numerical average.

[16.5pt] Required-to-Pass Capstone Completion Requirements
The final grade you will receive in the class is based on points accumulated during
the semester  and  on completion of required capstone deliverables. Thus, both
continued progress (the process) and the quality of your product (and other
deliverables) will determine your grade. Your grade is based on the performance of
your team and your individual contribution.
This course includes  non-negotiable completion requirements  that supersede
Canvas point totals.
To pass this course, a student  must :
1. Be a contributing member of a team that delivers a  working, demonstrable
system that meets the agreed-upon project scope  by the end of the
semester.
2. Participate in the  final project presentation and demonstration .
3. Submit all required  final deliverables , including:
Final project report, including all of the required components
Final videos, poster, and presentation slides
Final GitHub release
Required data and materials archives
4. Pass both the  written  and  oral communication  components of the course.
5. Return all equipment
Important:
A student cannot pass this course with a numerical average alone. Failure to meet
any required-to-pass condition will result in a course grade of F, regardless of
Canvas calculations.
Additionally, grade will reflect your peer reviews. Since a large number of
assignments are group created, your grade on those assignments will reflect your
percentage effort as reflected by your peer reviews and other effort monitoring
assignments. Specifically, your grade cannot be higher than the team grade on a
project, but it can be lower based on your participation in the project.

Capstone projects depend on consistent participation.
Students are expected to attend class, team meetings, sponsor meetings, and
scheduled presentations.
More than five unexcused absences  over the semester will result in
automatic failure of the course , as excessive absence undermines team
viability.
Excused absences must follow university policy and be communicated

This course emphasizes collaborative, professional teamwork.
Students will create and adhere to a  Team Agreement  outlining

Peer evaluations and contribution records (e.g., GitHub history) are used to
assess individual participation.
Students who fail to contribute meaningfully risk failing the course, even if the
team succeeds.

[16.0pt] Course Schedule
[12.9pt] Course Schedule Overview (Subject to Change)
Weeks 1–4:  Project Framing and Early Design
Students are introduced to senior capstone expectations and industry-sponsored
and faculty-proposed projects. Activities focus on identifying and formulating a
computing problem, understanding domain context and prior work, functioning
effectively in teams, ethical considerations, defining project goals, requirements,
and success criteria, and establishing project management foundations.
Assessment emphasizes iterative progress on early design, planning, and
communication deliverables.
Weeks 5–7:  Design Exploration and Evaluation Planning
Teams develop system designs derived from defined requirements and constraints.
Students learn to create evaluation plans aligned with their definition of success
and prepare for implementation through targeted skill development.
Assessment emphasizes progress on design, documentation, planning, and
evaluation preparation.
Weeks 8–11:  Implementation and Evaluation
Teams implement their systems and continue iterative evaluation, including testing
and review. User interface design and technical design decisions are communicated
and refined. User evaluation planning and execution begin during this phase.
Assessment emphasizes implementation progress, evaluation activities, and
technical documentation.
Weeks 12–14:  System Refinement and Presentation Preparation
Teams complete implementation and evaluation, analyze results, and determine
implications. Students prepare and deliver in-class and external presentations and
demonstrations.
Assessment emphasizes system quality, evaluation results, communication, and
professionalism.
Week 15:  Final Deliverables and Review
Teams finalize all project deliverables and participate in final presentations,
demonstrations, and reviews with industry or instructional staff.
Final assessment considers project completion, documentation, evaluation results,
and professional communication.

[16.0pt] Course Specific Attendance Policy
Attendance is important. Not attending weekly class meetings harms the other
members of your group and makes it much more difficult for the instructor to
assess your contributions to the group effort. Due to the undue burden your
frequent absences have on the rest of the team, more than five unexcused
absences are an automatic failure.
The instructors will evaluate your attendance through frequent quizzes, meetings,
participation in the discussions, participation in assignments, and/or contributions
to the team. Upon instructors’ discretion, arriving to class late (> 5 minutes late),
leaving early (> 5 minutes before class end), and/or not participating (e.g., not taking
a quiz, not being present for a status meeting, not submitting in class work, and/or
doing work not related to the class) can be considered an unexcused absence,
depending on the circumstances. Oversleeping, a term paper due, an exam to cram
for, etc., are not valid reasons. The instructor reserves the right to determine what
constitutes a “valid reason” on a case-by-case basis.
Absence / lateness during days in which teams may need to work in a remote
location to accomplish certain assignments (e.g., presenting your work outside of
class, interviewing experts, meeting with mentors, accessing technologies outside of
the classroom, etc.) count toward the 5 unexcused absences, unless the absence /
lateness are approved by the instructor in advance. Students are expected to meet
instructor’s requirements for check in, activity reporting requirements, and/or timely
submission of-class assignments.
You are responsible for monitoring / tracking your unexcused absences.
Students experiencing personal injury or illness that is too severe for the student to
attend class qualify for an excused absence (see Student Rule 7, Section 7.2.2)
assuming that documentationand notification guidelines are followed.
