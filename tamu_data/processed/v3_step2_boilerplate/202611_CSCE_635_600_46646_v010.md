[16.0pt] Catalog Description
AI Robotics. (3-1). Credit 3. Introduction and survey of artificial intelligence methods
for mobile robots (ground, aerial, or marine) for science and engineering majors;
theory and practice of unmanned systems, focusing on biological and cognitive
principles which differ from control theory formulations. Prerequisites: Graduate
classification.
[16.0pt] Course Learning Outcomes
Upon completion of this course, the learner will be able to:
Understand and explain core concepts in robotic autonomy, including state
estimation, planning, control, and decision making under uncertainty.
Compare and evaluate classical and learning-based methods used in modern
robotic systems, and understand their assumptions and limitations.
Apply mathematical and computational tools to analyze and design robot
decision-making and control pipelines.
Formulate and analyze robotic systems that interact with humans and
operate in open-world settings, accounting for uncertainty, real-time
constraints, and human-centered considerations.

[12.0pt] Description of Graded Work
In-class Pop Quizzes (3 total): No preparation is required, and answers will
not be graded for correctness. Full credit will be given as long as responses
are submitted within the required time.
Short Paper Reviews (10 total): Each week, a reading list of recommended
research papers will be provided. Over the course of the semester, students
are expected to select ten weeks in which they will submit a short review
(within 2 pages) based on two papers from that week’s list. Each review
should articulate the student’s understanding of the papers’ main
contributions, provide a critical analysis of their strengths and limitations, and
discuss open research questions or potential extensions. To support this
process, a set of guiding questions will be provided each week for reference.
Lead Paper Discussions (2 total): Each student will sign up for two dates
during the semester to lead class discussions on the assigned papers for that
week. This responsibility includes preparing slides or discussion prompts to
engage classmates and facilitating meaningful conversation around the
papers’ methodology, results, and broader implications.
Project Proposal and Short Presentation: Students will work individually or in
small teams (2 people) to select a project topic from a provided list (or
propose their own with instructor approval). Teams will submit a short written
proposal (within 2 pages) outlining the project’s goals, motivation, and
expected contributions. A brief in-class presentation will accompany the
proposal to gather initial feedback from the instructor and peers.
Midterm Project Check-in: A range of meeting slots will be provided for
students to choose from. Students will discuss their project progress and
anticipated timelines with the instructor in a brief oral check-in, optionally
supported by a small number of slides. The instructor will provide feedback
to help guide the remainder of the project.
Final Project Presentation and Report: At the end of the semester, each team
will present their final project results in a formal presentation. A written
project report (6–8 pages) summarizing the problem, methodology,
experiments, and findings will be submitted as part of the final evaluation.
