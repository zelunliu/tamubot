Biography
Dr. Nancy Currie-Gregg is the Director of the Texas A&M University Space Institute.
As the Don Lummus '58 Professor of Practice in Engineering, she holds joint
appointments in the Departments of Aerospace Engineering and Industrial and
Systems Engineering at Texas A&M University's College of Engineering.
Internationally recognized for her human-system integration, safety engineering,
and risk management expertise, her research focuses on optimizing human
performance and safety in aerospace systems. She also serves on the Board of
Directors for the Texas Space Commission, leading initiatives that promote growth
across all sectors of the Texas space economy.
Dr. Currie-Gregg spent the majority of her career supporting NASA's human
spaceflight programs. A former NASA astronaut, she flew on four space shuttle
missions, logging over 1,000 hours in space, and made significant contributions to
the development of human spaceflight operations. Following the Space Shuttle
Columbia tragedy, she managed the Space Shuttle Program Safety and Mission
Assurance Office, overseeing the agency's return-to-flight efforts. She later served
as Principal Engineer and Chief Engineer with the NASA Engineering and Safety
Center, where she led independent engineering assessments for high-risk human
spaceflight initiatives. A retired U.S. Army Colonel and Master Army Aviator, she
accrued more than 4,000 flight hours in both rotary- and fixed-wing aircraft.
Dr. Currie-Gregg holds a Bachelor's degree in Biological Sciences with honors from
The Ohio State University, a Master of Science in Safety Engineering from the
University of Southern California, and a Ph.D. in Industrial Engineering from the
University of Houston.

[16.0pt] Catalog Description
Human Factors Engineering for Aerospace Designs. (3-0). Credit 3. Physiological and
psychophysiological issues encountered in aviation and space environments and
their effect on human cognitive and physical performance; survey methods for
human workload and performance evaluations; apply human-systems design
principles to an aerospace design project. Prerequisites: Graduate classification or
approval of instructor. Cross-listed with AERO 636.

[16.0pt] Course Learning Outcomes
Upon completion of this course, the learner will be able to:
Analyze how human sensory, motor, and cognitive attributes may influence
human performance in the operation of aviation and space systems.
Discuss principle physiological and psychophysiological concerns related to
aviation and spaceflight environments and evaluate their potential impact on
human performance.
Formulate and evaluate principled hypotheses for human-system design
optimization and improvement.
Critique published literature on physiological and psychophysiological issues
and evaluate proposed mitigations and countermeasures.
Develop solutions to current human-system integration challenges for
aerospace applications.
Assess proposed design solutions from the perspective of human
performance to include physical/cognitive workload, attention, situation
awareness, and mission performance.
Synthesize and effectively communicate attributes of the proposed design
solutions to include specific human-system integration requirements, system
characteristics, and human performance assessment results.

Course Project
A crucial aspect of the course is participation in a group project to design an aircraft
or spacecraft system that enhances operator effectiveness, usability, and safety.
This project aims to deepen comprehension and understanding by applying course
concepts and methods to create systems that account for human capabilities and
limitations in aerospace environments.
The instructor will form teams based on each student's assessment of their
interests and capabilities. Each team will select an aerospace human-system design
project (e.g., a situational awareness display, rover controller, personal restraint
system, payload workstation, etc.) and research the need for a solution, develop a
proposed design, construct a prototype, and test or evaluate the prototype using
the techniques and methods discussed in this course.
There will be several interim deliverables throughout the semester to ensure
progress on the project. These include a design concept of operations, HSI design
requirements, and human-in-the-loop testing and evaluation criteria. The final
deliverables for the course project are a prototype, an oral presentation by the
project team, and a final report. All team members must attend and participate in
the presentation. Distance-learning students may pre-record their portion of the
presentation or participate virtually, via Zoom, during the scheduled presentation.
Additionally, there will be an award for the best design, as determined by an expert
panel. The due dates for all deliverables are provided in the detailed course
schedule.
Details regarding specific formats and requirements for all course project
deliverables will be covered during lectures and posted on Canvas. Additionally,
detailed grading rubrics for all deliverables will be provided and posted well in
advance of the due dates. All students in the group will receive the same score on
the project, unless indicated otherwise based on peer evaluations.

[16.0pt] AI Statement
The primary goal of this course is for students to understand the effects of
aerospace environments on human health and performance, and to formulate
design approaches to mitigate these effects. Judicious and ethical use of Artificial
Intelligence (AI) tools can assist with initial comprehension and practice of course
concepts. Limited use of AI is therefore permitted to support student learning, but
not to replace a student’s original work.
Examples of  permitted use  of AI agents include the following:
To assist with comprehension of course material or concepts
The use of AI-based tools to further explore concepts presented in the
course is encouraged. For example, AI tools may be used to provide
alternative explanations of concepts presented in lecture materials or
textbooks. AI may be used as a study aid, similar to tutoring, but not to
generate material that is submitted for academic credit.
To assist with the readability of homework or project assignments
Use of AI tools to revise and edit a student’s own writing to improve clarity,
organization, or readability is permitted. However, using an AI tool to
generate responses, analyses, calculations, or written content for homework
or project assignments is not permitted, even if the output is subsequently
modified.
Instructions for each homework or project assignment will specify whether and how
AI-based tools may be used for that assignment. If any AI tools are used at any
stage during completion of an assignment, students must properly cite the use of AI
and submit the following information with the assignment:
The name of the AI tool and the specific version used
A brief explanation of why assistance from the AI tool was sought
Any prompts used, provided exactly as input to the tool, enclosed in
quotation marks.
Students should be aware that AI-generated results may be inaccurate, incomplete,
or biased. Students are personally responsible for verifying the accuracy of any
information obtained from AI tools. Additionally, copyrighted materials (including
lecture materials) and personal or sensitive information should not be shared
unintentionally when interacting with AI agents.
Use of AI assistants during any portion of a quiz is strictly prohibited .
Any use of AI assistance during a quiz will be considered academic misconduct
under Rule 20.1.2.2.1 (“Cheating”) and a violation of the Aggie Honor Code. See the
Aggie Honor System Office for additional information.
Questions regarding citation, academic integrity, or acceptable use of AI in this
course should be directed to the instructor  prior to  the assignment submission.
